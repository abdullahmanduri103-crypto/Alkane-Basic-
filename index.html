Organic Compounds Classification: Fundamental Concepts

What is Organic Chemistry?

Organic chemistry is the study of carbon-containing compounds. Carbon's unique ability to form four covalent bonds allows it to create millions of different compounds. Understanding classification helps in predicting properties and reactions.

Key Concept: Two Classification Methods

1. Based on Functional Groups: Compounds with same functional group show similar chemical properties. 2. Based on Structure: Open chain (acyclic) vs closed chain (cyclic).

MDCAT/NUMS Focus Areas:

  • Identifying functional groups in given compounds
  • Classifying compounds as aliphatic/aromatic
  • Differentiating between isomers
  • Recognizing homologous series
  • Understanding IUPAC naming basics

Competition Level Exceptional Cases:

  1. Ambiguous compounds: Some compounds belong to multiple classes (e.g., phenol has -OH and aromatic ring)
  2. Bridged compounds: Compounds with bridges between rings
  3. Heterocyclic compounds: Rings containing atoms other than carbon
  4. Tautomers: Compounds that exist in equilibrium between two structures
MCQ Solving Trick: Functional Group Priority

Remember priority order for naming: Carboxylic acid > Ester > Amide > Aldehyde > Ketone > Alcohol > Amine > Alkene > Alkyne > Alkane. For MDCAT, focus on recognizing groups rather than complex naming.

Direct MCQ Points:

  1. Aliphatic = open chain, Aromatic = benzene ring containing
  2. Saturated = only single bonds, Unsaturated = double/triple bonds
  3. Functional group determines chemical behavior
  4. Homologous series: Same functional group, differ by -CH₂-
  5. Isomers: Same molecular formula, different structures

Detailed MCQ Explanation Example:

Sample MCQ with Detailed Analysis

Question: Which compound is both aromatic and contains alcohol functional group?

Options: A) Ethanol, B) Phenol, C) Methanol, D) Benzene

Step-by-step analysis:

  1. Ethanol: Aliphatic alcohol (C₂H₅OH)
  2. Phenol: C₆H₅OH (benzene ring + -OH group)
  3. Methanol: CH₃OH (simplest alcohol)
  4. Benzene: C₆H₆ (aromatic but no -OH group)
  5. Only phenol has both aromatic ring and alcohol (-OH) group
  6. Thus answer: B) Phenol

Learning Trick: "Phenol = Phenyl + Alcohol" - remember this mnemonic for aromatic alcohols.

Conceptual Understanding (5/25)

Why is carbon unique in forming so many compounds?
Answer: Carbon has 4 valence electrons, can form 4 covalent bonds (tetravalency). It can form strong C-C bonds (catenation). It can form single, double, triple bonds. Small size allows effective overlap. Forms stable bonds with H, O, N, S, halogens.
What's difference between aliphatic and aromatic?
Answer: Aliphatic: Open chain or non-aromatic cyclic compounds. Can be saturated (alkanes) or unsaturated (alkenes/alkynes). Aromatic: Contain benzene ring or similar planar ring with (4n+2)π electrons (Huckel's rule). Aromatic compounds show resonance.
What is homologous series? Give example.
Answer: Series of compounds with same functional group, each member differs by -CH₂- unit. Example: Alkanes (CH₄, C₂H₆, C₃H₈...). Properties change gradually: boiling point increases, density increases with molecular weight.
What are functional groups? Why important?
Answer: Functional group = atom or group of atoms that determines chemical properties. Examples: -OH (alcohol), -CHO (aldehyde), -COOH (carboxylic acid). Same functional group gives similar reactions. Organic chemistry organized around functional groups.
Can organic compound be ionic?
Answer: Yes, but rare. Most organic compounds covalent. Examples: Carboxylate ions (RCOO⁻), ammonium salts (RNH₃⁺). Soaps (sodium salts of fatty acids) are ionic organic compounds. Most MDCAT questions assume covalent bonding.

Classification MCQs (5)

Alkanes and Cycloalkanes: Saturated Hydrocarbons

Alkanes (Paraffins):

General formula: CₙH₂ₙ₊₂. Saturated hydrocarbons with only C-C and C-H single bonds. Non-polar, insoluble in water, less dense than water. Undergo substitution reactions.

Key Concept: Physical Properties Trend

Boiling point increases with molecular weight. Branched isomers have lower boiling points than straight-chain (less surface area). Methane (CH₄) to Butane (C₄H₁₀) = gases, Pentane (C₅H₁₂) to C₁₇H₃₆ = liquids, Higher = solids.

Cycloalkanes:

General formula: CₙH₂ₙ (same as alkenes but saturated). Ring structures. Angle strain in small rings (cyclopropane, cyclobutane). Chair conformation in cyclohexane is strain-free.

MCQ Solving Trick: Isomer Counting

For MDCAT, remember: Methane (1), Ethane (1), Propane (1), Butane (2), Pentane (3), Hexane (5). Cycloalkanes: Cyclopropane (1), Cyclobutane (2), Cyclopentane (1), Cyclohexane (1). For cycloalkanes with substituents, consider cis-trans isomerism.

MDCAT Problem Solving Strategy:

  1. For boiling point comparisons: More branching = lower boiling point
  2. For isomer counting: Draw systematically or use known values
  3. For reactivity: Tertiary H > Secondary H > Primary H in halogenation
  4. For stability: More substituted carbocations are more stable

Detailed MCQ Explanation Example:

Sample MCQ with Detailed Analysis

Question: Which alkane has highest boiling point?

Options: A) n-Pentane, B) 2-Methylbutane, C) 2,2-Dimethylpropane, D) All same

Step-by-step solution:

  1. All are isomers: C₅H₁₂
  2. n-Pentane: Straight chain, maximum surface area
  3. 2-Methylbutane: One branch, less surface area
  4. 2,2-Dimethylpropane: Highly branched, minimum surface area
  5. Boiling point order: Straight chain > Branched (more branching = lower bp)
  6. Thus n-Pentane has highest boiling point → Answer: A

Learning Trick: "Straight chains stick together better" - more surface contact = stronger London forces = higher boiling point.

Direct MCQ Points:

  1. Alkanes: CₙH₂ₙ₊₂, Cycloalkanes: CₙH₂ₙ
  2. Branched alkanes have lower boiling points
  3. Cyclopropane has highest angle strain (60° vs 109.5° ideal)
  4. Cyclohexane chair conformation is most stable
  5. Alkanes undergo free radical substitution

Conceptual Understanding (10/25)

Why are alkanes called paraffins?
Answer: From Latin "parum affinis" = little affinity. Alkanes are relatively unreactive due to strong C-C and C-H bonds (non-polar). They don't react with acids, bases, or oxidizing agents under normal conditions. Only react with halogens under UV light (free radical substitution).
Why does boiling point increase with molecular weight?
Answer: Larger molecules have more electrons → stronger London dispersion forces (temporary dipole-induced dipole). Also, larger surface area for contact between molecules. Energy needed to separate molecules increases.
What is angle strain in cycloalkanes?
Answer: Deviation of bond angles from ideal tetrahedral angle (109.5°). Cyclopropane: 60° (high strain, reactive). Cyclobutane: 90° (moderate strain). Cyclopentane: 108° (almost ideal). Cyclohexane: 109.5° (chair conformation, no strain).

IUPAC Nomenclature of Alkanes and Cycloalkanes

Basic Rules of IUPAC Nomenclature:

1. Identify longest continuous carbon chain (parent chain)
2. Number chain to give lowest numbers to substituents
3. Name substituents in alphabetical order
4. Use prefixes di-, tri-, tetra- for identical substituents
5. For cycloalkanes: Use "cyclo" prefix

Key Concept: Numbering Priority

When multiple options: Give lowest number to first substituent. If tie: Give lowest number to second substituent. If functional group present: It gets priority in numbering. For alkenes/alkynes: Double/triple bond gets lowest number.

Common Alkyl Groups (MDCAT Must Know):

  • Methyl: -CH₃ (from methane)
  • Ethyl: -C₂H₅ (from ethane)
  • Propyl: -C₃H₇ (two types: n-propyl and isopropyl)
  • Butyl: -C₄H₉ (n-butyl, sec-butyl, isobutyl, tert-butyl)
  • Pentyl/Amyl: -C₅H₁₁
MCQ Solving Trick: Common vs IUPAC Names

For MDCAT, know both common and IUPAC names. Isopropyl = 2-propyl, tert-butyl = 2-methyl-2-propyl, iso-butyl = 2-methylpropyl. Vinyl = ethenyl, Allyl = 2-propenyl, Phenyl = C₆H₅-.

Detailed MCQ Explanation Example:

Sample MCQ with Detailed Analysis

Question: IUPAC name of (CH₃)₂CHCH₂CH(CH₃)₂ is:

Options: A) 2,4-Dimethylpentane, B) 1,1,3,3-Tetramethylpropane, C) 2-Methylbutane, D) Isohexane

Step-by-step solution:

  1. Draw structure: CH₃-CH(CH₃)-CH₂-CH(CH₃)-CH₃
  2. Longest chain: 5 carbons (pentane)
  3. Number from left: Methyl at C2 and C4
  4. Number from right: Same result (methyl at C2 and C4)
  5. Name: 2,4-Dimethylpentane
  6. Thus answer: A) 2,4-Dimethylpentane

Learning Trick: Count carbons systematically: Start from one end, assign numbers, check if other direction gives lower numbers.

Direct MCQ Points:

  1. Methane (1C), Ethane (2C), Propane (3C), Butane (4C), Pentane (5C)
  2. Hexane (6C), Heptane (7C), Octane (8C), Nonane (9C), Decane (10C)
  3. Cycloalkanes: cyclopropane, cyclobutane, cyclopentane, cyclohexane
  4. For disubstituted cycloalkanes: cis (same side) vs trans (opposite side)
  5. Isopropyl group: -CH(CH₃)₂

Conceptual Understanding (15/25)

Why is IUPAC naming necessary?
Answer: Common names ambiguous (e.g., "isobutane" could mean different things). IUPAC gives unique name for each compound. Systematic rules allow naming of complex molecules. Essential for chemical communication worldwide.
What's difference between iso, sec, tert prefixes?
Answer: iso-: One branch at second carbon from end (e.g., isobutane = (CH₃)₂CHCH₃). sec- (secondary): Carbon attached to two other carbons (e.g., sec-butyl = CH₃CH₂CH(CH₃)-). tert- (tertiary): Carbon attached to three other carbons (e.g., tert-butyl = (CH₃)₃C-).
How to name cycloalkanes with substituents?
Answer: 1. Use "cyclo" prefix. 2. Number substituents to get lowest set of numbers. 3. If only one substituent, no number needed. 4. If two substituents: number them 1 and 2. 5. For cis-trans: cis = same side, trans = opposite sides.

Nomenclature MCQs (5)

Structure of Ethane: Conformations and Hybridization

Ethane (C₂H₆) Structure:

Simplest alkane with C-C bond. Each carbon sp³ hybridized, tetrahedral geometry (109.5°). Rotation possible around C-C single bond → different conformations.

Key Concept: Conformational Isomers

Different arrangements due to rotation around single bond. Not separable at room temperature (interconvert rapidly). Staggered conformation most stable (minimum electron repulsion). Eclipsed conformation least stable (maximum electron repulsion).

Energy Diagram for Ethane Conformations:

Staggered: Minimum energy (0 kJ/mol). Eclipsed: Maximum energy (~12 kJ/mol). Energy barrier for rotation = 12 kJ/mol (small, so free rotation at room temperature).

MDCAT Trick: Newman Projection

Front carbon represented by dot, back carbon by circle. Lines represent bonds. Staggered: bonds 60° apart. Eclipsed: bonds aligned. Gauche: Special staggered with 60° angle between large groups.

Detailed MCQ Explanation Example:

Sample MCQ with Detailed Analysis

Question: In ethane, staggered conformation is more stable than eclipsed because:

Options: A) Less steric hindrance, B) More hydrogen bonding, C) Stronger C-C bond, D) Lower molecular weight

Step-by-step analysis:

  1. Option A: Steric hindrance = repulsion between atoms/groups
  2. In staggered: H atoms maximally separated (60° apart)
  3. In eclipsed: H atoms directly aligned (0° apart)
  4. Aligned H atoms in eclipsed cause electron-electron repulsion
  5. No hydrogen bonding in ethane (only C-H bonds)
  6. C-C bond strength same in both conformations
  7. Molecular weight same in both
  8. Thus answer: A) Less steric hindrance

Learning Trick: "Staggered = Stable, Eclipsed = Energy" - remember S-S and E-E pairs.

Direct MCQ Points:

  1. Ethane: C₂H₆, each C sp³ hybridized
  2. Staggered conformation: Most stable, H atoms 60° apart
  3. Eclipsed conformation: Least stable, H atoms aligned
  4. Energy difference: ~12 kJ/mol between staggered/eclipsed
  5. Free rotation at room temperature (energy barrier low)

Conceptual Understanding (20/25)

What is hybridization of carbon in ethane?
Answer: sp³ hybridization. Each carbon has 4 sp³ hybrid orbitals (25% s, 75% p character). Forms 4 σ bonds: 3 C-H bonds and 1 C-C bond. Bond angles ~109.5° (tetrahedral). Free rotation around C-C σ bond.
Why free rotation in ethane but not in ethene?
Answer: Ethane has C-C single bond (σ bond), cylindrical symmetry allows rotation. Ethene has C=C double bond (1σ + 1π bond). π bond has electron density above and below plane, rotation would break π bond (requires ~270 kJ/mol).
What is torsional strain in ethane?
Answer: Repulsion between bonding electrons when bonds eclipse each other. In eclipsed conformation: electron clouds of C-H bonds repel. Causes 12 kJ/mol energy barrier. Also called Pitzer strain or eclipsing strain.

Structure of Cyclopropane and Cyclobutane: Angle Strain

Cyclopropane (C₃H₆):

Smallest cycloalkane. Equilateral triangle with 60° bond angles (vs 109.5° ideal). High angle strain. C-C bonds bent outward (banana bonds). More reactive than open-chain alkanes.

Key Concept: Banana Bonds in Cyclopropane

C-C bonds in cyclopropane are not normal σ bonds. They are bent orbitals (banana bonds) with more p-character. Overlap is poor → weak bonds. Electron density outside ring → unusual reactivity.

Cyclobutane (C₄H₆):

Square planar would have 90° angles, but actually puckered (folded) to reduce strain. Bond angles ~88° (less strain than cyclopropane). More stable than cyclopropane but still strained.

MDCAT Trick: Stability Order

Cyclohexane (chair) > Cyclopentane > Cyclobutane > Cyclopropane (least stable). Remember: "6>5>4>3" for stability. For angle strain: Cyclopropane (49.5° deviation) > Cyclobutane (21.5°) > Cyclopentane (1.5°) > Cyclohexane (0°).

Detailed MCQ Explanation Example:

Sample MCQ with Detailed Analysis

Question: Which cycloalkane has highest angle strain?

Options: A) Cyclopropane, B) Cyclobutane, C) Cyclopentane, D) Cyclohexane

Step-by-step analysis:

  1. Ideal tetrahedral angle: 109.5°
  2. Cyclopropane: 60° (deviation = 49.5°)
  3. Cyclobutane: ~90° (deviation = 19.5°)
  4. Cyclopentane: ~108° (deviation = 1.5°)
  5. Cyclohexane (chair): 109.5° (deviation = 0°)
  6. Largest deviation = highest strain
  7. Thus cyclopropane has highest angle strain → Answer: A

Learning Trick: "Small rings = Big strain" - the smaller the ring, the greater the deviation from 109.5°.

Direct MCQ Points:

  1. Cyclopropane: 60° angles, banana bonds, most strained
  2. Cyclobutane: Puckered, ~88° angles, less strained than cyclopropane
  3. Cyclopentane: Almost ideal angles (108°), envelope conformation
  4. Cyclohexane: Chair conformation, 109.5° angles, strain-free
  5. Strain energy: Cyclopropane > Cyclobutane > Cyclopentane > Cyclohexane

Conceptual Understanding (25/25)

Why is cyclopropane reactive despite being alkane?
Answer: High angle strain (60° vs 109.5° ideal). Weak "banana bonds" with poor overlap. Ring strain makes it behave like alkene in some reactions (addition reactions to relieve strain). Can undergo ring-opening reactions easily.
Why is cyclobutane puckered, not planar?
Answer: Puckering reduces two types of strain: 1) Angle strain: Bonds slightly bend from 90°. 2) Torsional strain: In planar form, all H atoms would be eclipsed. Puckering reduces eclipsing interactions, lowering energy by ~6 kJ/mol.
What is Baeyer's strain theory?
Answer: Proposed by Adolf von Baeyer (1885). Cycloalkanes with 5-6 carbons most stable (angles near 109.5°). Smaller or larger rings have angle strain. Limitation: Assumed all rings planar, didn't consider puckering or other conformations.

Electrophile, Nucleophile, Free Radical, Bond Fission

Electrophile (Electron-Loving):

Electron-deficient species, seeks electrons. Positively charged or neutral with partial positive charge. Examples: H⁺, Cl⁺, Br⁺, NO₂⁺, SO₃, BF₃, AlCl₃. Attack electron-rich sites (nucleophiles).

Key Concept: Identifying Electrophiles

Look for: Positive charge, incomplete octet, polarized bonds where atom is δ⁺. Common electrophiles in organic chemistry: Carbocations (R₃C⁺), carbonyl carbon (C=O δ⁺), proton (H⁺).

Nucleophile (Nucleus-Loving):

Electron-rich species, donates electrons. Negatively charged or neutral with lone pairs. Examples: OH⁻, CN⁻, NH₃, H₂O, ROH, RNH₂. Attack electron-deficient sites (electrophiles).

MDCAT Trick: Nucleophile Strength

Stronger nucleophile: More negative charge, smaller size, less solvated. Order in polar protic solvents: I⁻ > Br⁻ > Cl⁻ > F⁻ (polarizability effect). In polar aprotic: F⁻ > Cl⁻ > Br⁻ > I⁻ (size effect).

Bond Fission Types:

Homolytic: Bond breaks equally, one electron to each atom. Produces free radicals. Requires energy (heat/light). Example: Cl-Cl → 2Cl•
Heterolytic: Bond breaks unequally, both electrons go to one atom. Produces ions (cation + anion). Example: H-Cl → H⁺ + Cl⁻

Detailed MCQ Explanation Example:

Sample MCQ with Detailed Analysis

Question: Which is NOT an electrophile?

Options: A) H⁺, B) Cl⁺, C) OH⁻, D) BF₃

Step-by-step analysis:

  1. H⁺: Proton, positive charge, electron-deficient → Electrophile ✓
  2. Cl⁺: Chlorine cation, positive charge, electron-deficient → Electrophile ✓
  3. OH⁻: Hydroxide ion, negative charge, electron-rich → Nucleophile ✗
  4. BF₃: Boron trifluoride, incomplete octet (6 electrons), electron-deficient → Electrophile ✓
  5. Thus OH⁻ is not an electrophile → Answer: C

Learning Trick: "Electrophile = Likes electrons (positive/δ⁺), Nucleophile = Likes nucleus (negative/lone pairs)". OH⁻ has negative charge and lone pairs = nucleophile.

Direct MCQ Points:

  1. Electrophile: Electron-deficient, positive/δ⁺
  2. Nucleophile: Electron-rich, negative/lone pairs
  3. Free radical: Unpaired electron, neutral
  4. Homolytic fission: Produces radicals, symmetrical
  5. Heterolytic fission: Produces ions, unsymmetrical

Conceptual Understanding Review

What determines if species is electrophile or nucleophile?
Answer: Electron density: Electrophiles lack electrons (seek electrons). Nucleophiles have excess electrons (donate electrons). Charge: Positive = electrophile, Negative = nucleophile. Structure: Incomplete octet = electrophile, Lone pairs = nucleophile.
Why is homolytic fission common in non-polar bonds?
Answer: Non-polar bonds (Cl-Cl, Br-Br) have equal electron sharing. Break symmetrically → one electron to each atom. Requires UV light or heat (radical initiators). Polar bonds (H-Cl) break heterolytically because electrons drawn to more electronegative atom.
What are free radicals? Give examples.
Answer: Species with unpaired electron. Highly reactive, short-lived intermediates. Examples: Methyl radical (CH₃•), Chlorine radical (Cl•). Formed by homolytic fission or redox reactions. Stabilized by resonance or hyperconjugation.

Reactive Intermediates MCQs (5)

Free Radical Substitution: Chlorination of Methane & Ethene

Chlorination of Methane (Free Radical Substitution):

Three Steps: 1) Initiation: Cl₂ → 2Cl• (UV light)
2) Propagation: Cl• + CH₄ → HCl + CH₃•; CH₃• + Cl₂ → CH₃Cl + Cl•
3) Termination: Various radical combinations

Key Concept: Selectivity in Halogenation

Reactivity of H atoms: Tertiary (3°) > Secondary (2°) > Primary (1°). Relative rates at 25°C: 1°H:1, 2°H:3.8, 3°H:5.0. Chlorination less selective than bromination. For methane: Only primary H, so only CH₃Cl formed (but can give poly chlorinated products).

Chlorination of Ethene (Electrophilic Addition):

Different mechanism! Ethene has π bond, not alkane. Electrophilic addition, not free radical substitution. Mechanism: Cl₂ polarized → Cl⁺ attacks π bond → carbocation → Cl⁻ attacks carbocation → 1,2-dichloroethane.

MDCAT Trick: Major/Minor Products

For unsymmetrical alkenes (e.g., propene): Markovnikov's rule applies. H adds to less substituted carbon, Cl to more substituted carbon. For free radical substitution on alkanes: More substituted radicals form more easily (stability order: 3° > 2° > 1° > methyl).

Detailed MCQ Explanation Example:

Sample MCQ with Detailed Analysis

Question: In chlorination of methane with excess Cl₂, the major final product is:

Options: A) CH₃Cl, B) CH₂Cl₂, C) CHCl₃, D) CCl₄

Step-by-step analysis:

  1. Free radical substitution gives mixture: CH₃Cl, CH₂Cl₂, CHCl₃, CCl₄
  2. With excess chlorine and continuous reaction:
  3. CH₃Cl formed first, but can react further: CH₃Cl + Cl• → •CH₂Cl + HCl
  4. •CH₂Cl + Cl₂ → CH₂Cl₂ + Cl• (and so on)
  5. Eventually, all H atoms replaced by Cl
  6. With large excess Cl₂, CCl₄ is major product
  7. Thus answer: D) CCl₄

Learning Trick: "Excess Cl₂ → Complete chlorination" - all H atoms eventually replaced if Cl₂ is in excess.

Direct MCQ Points:

  1. Methane chlorination: Free radical substitution, requires UV light
  2. Ethene chlorination: Electrophilic addition, occurs in dark
  3. Free radical stability: 3° > 2° > 1° > methyl
  4. For alkanes with different H types: mixture of products
  5. For CH₄: CH₃Cl → CH₂Cl₂ → CHCl₃ → CCl₄ (with excess Cl₂)

Conceptual Understanding Review

Why UV light needed for methane chlorination but not ethene chlorination?
Answer: Methane chlorination: Free radical mechanism needs Cl• radicals from Cl₂ homolysis (requires UV light). Ethene chlorination: Electrophilic addition via polar Cl⁺ (from polarized Cl₂), no radicals needed, occurs without light.
What controls product ratio in propane chlorination?
Answer: Two factors: 1) Number of each type of H (6 primary, 2 secondary). 2) Reactivity of each H type (secondary 3.8× more reactive than primary). Product ratio = (number of H) × (relative reactivity). For propane: (6×1) primary vs (2×3.8) secondary.
Why is free radical substitution chain reaction?
Answer: Propagation steps regenerate radicals. Cl• consumed in step 1 but regenerated in step 2. One Cl• can cause conversion of many CH₄ molecules. Chain length = number of cycles before termination. Typical chain length ~10⁴ for chlorination.

MCQ Solving Strategy for Organic Chemistry
  1. Identify the concept: Is it about nomenclature, structure, mechanism, or properties?
  2. Draw if possible: Visualize structures for isomer questions
  3. Eliminate absurd options: Remove chemically impossible answers
  4. Apply rules systematically: Use IUPAC rules, stability orders, reaction mechanisms

Common MCQ Patterns in Pakistani Exams:

  • IUPAC naming: Given structure → find correct name
  • Isomer counting: How many isomers for given formula?
  • Stability comparison: Which conformer/carbocation/radical is most stable?
  • Reaction products: What is major product of given reaction?
  • Concept identification: Which is electrophile/nucleophile/radical?
  • Hybridization: What is hybridization of specific atom?
  • Physical properties: Boiling point/melting point/solubility trends
Advanced Level: Mechanism-Based Questions

Free radical substitution: Identify initiation/propagation/termination steps. Electrophilic addition: Apply Markovnikov's rule. Carbocation stability: 3° > 2° > 1° > methyl. Radical stability: Same order as carbocations. Steric effects: Bulkier groups affect reactivity.

Detailed MCQ Explanation Example (Advanced Level):

Sample Advanced MCQ with Detailed Analysis

Question: Chlorination of 2-methylbutane gives four monochloro products. Which will be formed in least amount?

Options: A) 1-chloro-2-methylbutane, B) 2-chloro-2-methylbutane, C) 2-chloro-3-methylbutane, D) 1-chloro-3-methylbutane

Step-by-step solution:

  1. Structure: CH₃-CH(CH₃)-CH₂-CH₃ (2-methylbutane)
  2. Types of H: 9 primary (3 types), 2 secondary, 1 tertiary
  3. Reactivity: 3°H (5.0) > 2°H (3.8) > 1°H (1.0)
  4. Product from tertiary H: 2-chloro-2-methylbutane (B)
  5. Secondary H: gives 2-chloro-3-methylbutane (C) and 3-chloro-2-methylbutane
  6. Primary H: three types give different products
  7. Least amount: From least reactive H with fewest numbers
  8. Primary H on C1 (9 in number but low reactivity) vs other primary H
  9. Actually, statistical distribution × reactivity gives 1-chloro-3-methylbutane as least
  10. Thus answer: D) 1-chloro-3-methylbutane

Learning Trick: For free radical halogenation: Amount ∝ (number of H) × (relative reactivity). Calculate for each type.
